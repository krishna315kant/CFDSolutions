Solver Development

Numerical methods play a crucial role in understanding multiphase flows, which are prevalent in various industrial and natural processes. A key challenge in multiphase simulations is minimizing numerical artifacts such as spurious currents, which can significantly affect result accuracy.

My research focuses on improving numerical solvers for multiphase flows by developing robust methods to reduce spurious currents. During my Ph.D., I implemented a smoothing technique combined with a close-point search method to enhance interface stability, reducing spurious currents by 2â€“3 orders of magnitude. These advancements have contributed to more accurate predictions of phase interfaces, surface tension effects, and droplet interactions.

Future Directions

Ongoing work includes extending these methods to turbulent multiphase flows and coupling them with sub-grid scale methods through machine learning techniques to improve computational efficiency.

Atomization

Atomization is a fundamental process in spray systems, affecting combustion efficiency, fuel economy, and pollutant formation. A critical parameter in predicting droplet breakup is the Weber number, which governs the balance between inertial and surface tension forces.

My Ph.D. research focused on developing a generalized correlation for the critical Weber number across a wide range of density ratios and Ohnesorge numbers. Additionally, I contributed to improving internal flow theory, enabling more accurate predictions of droplet deformation rates, maximum deformation, and breakup mechanisms in atomizing flows.

Future Directions

Future research aims to refine breakup models through high-fidelity simulations and experimental validation, incorporating non-Newtonian effects and compressibility influences.

Flash Boiling

Flash boiling occurs when a liquid undergoes rapid phase change due to sudden depressurization, leading to enhanced atomization and vaporization. This phenomenon is critical in fuel injection systems, cryogenic storage, and space applications.

In my postdoctoral research, I am working with CONVERGE CFD and the Real Fluid Model to develop predictive models for metastable states in flash boiling. This involves capturing superheated liquid behavior and bubble nucleation mechanisms to improve phase change modeling in extreme conditions.

Future Directions

Upcoming work includes extending flash boiling models to transcritical regimes and integrating them with multiphase turbulence models for real-world applications.

Quantum Computing for Fluid Simulations

Quantum computing offers promising advancements in solving complex fluid flow problems, particularly in high-dimensional simulations where classical computing faces limitations.

Currently, I am exploring quantum computing fundamentals through a foundational course at QpiAI. My objective is to investigate how quantum-based solvers can improve computational efficiency in multiphase and turbulence modeling, potentially enabling faster and more accurate simulations.

Future Directions

Next steps involve evaluating quantum algorithms for solving Navier-Stokes equations and assessing their feasibility for large-scale industrial applications.
